Project: How are Emerging Economies transitioning to Renewable energy?

For my project I investigated how BRICS and MINT economies are transitioning to renewable energy. This question is important due to the necessity renewables in preventing climate change and to accelerate it, understanding the mechanics are paramount. I address 3 issues, how usage of renewables is growing, the relation between economic growth and renewables and the effect of depletion of fossil fuels on renewables.

Both processing and analysis were handled through a central Colab workbook projectPython.pynb to centralise my work and to make it easier to spot bugs in my code I mainly accessed my data through the World Bank API Package in Python, allowing me to gain access to a range of different databases and time series for my chosen countries, whilst also saving my requests as Python code, improving reproducibility. Furthermore, the package had built in integration with Pandas, which I used throughout to clean and format my data.

My variables of interest were percentage of energy output from renewables, GDP per capita and energy depletion savings (%GNI), which were saved as a list. This list was used to create a Pandas Dataframe for my chosen economies between 2000 and 2020, allowing manipulation such as rounding values, renaming headers and handling of missing values. This processing was done to allow for the data frame to easily be inserted into VegaLite for visualisation and SciKitLearn for regression. I also used data from the UNSDGâ€™s to measure international financial flows into renewables which had to be manually downloaded and added to the data set through a merge within Pandas, requiring alignment of indexes and key value pairs. This data was then converted to CSV and after addressing missing values, was downloaded and posted to GitHub.

3 time-series graphs were produced in VegaLite, showing GDP, proportion of renewables and financial flows for my chosen countries, however due to lack of data, Russia was excluded from financial flows. These showed temporal data in dates, quantitative in measurements and categorical in countries. Improve readability given the amount of series on the graph, an interactive overlay tooltip was used which showed the exact values along the series.

Interestingly, GDP per capita was shown to grow as well as the financial flows, possibly highlighting both the wider importance on renewables and ensuring expansion of capacity as energy demand grows. However the proportion of renewables wildly differed between countries, with some like South Africa (ZAR) having close to 0, whilst Nigeria (NGA) obtained over 80% from renewables. Further, the direction of the gradient varied, with some countries share expanding and others falling, indicating country specific drivers likely play a highly significant effect.

To take the analysis further, I wanted to directly compare renewable generation with GDP per capita to see how energy mix changed when countries became richer and with Energy Depletion Savings to see if remaining stocks of fossil fuels as a proportion of Gross National income effected renewable generation. I chose to use the SciKitLearn linear regression package to do this. This required further manipulation of the data, in converting my DataFrame to fit the 2D requirement of SkLearn, which was done with the reshape() method and dropping of missing values. With this, the data was loaded and fitted, outputting coefficients for my data.

Whilst I could have visualised with matplotlib, I chose to continue with VegaLite for consistency, which required generating a regression from the scatter plot that was made to visualise the relationships. This involved using a regression transform and I also calculated R-squared, showing the GDP per capita had greater explanatory power than depletion, which was near zero at 0.01. That said, By colouring countries, I could see clusters in both charts indicating potentially more powerful relationships for further investigation. This clustering implies an individual data generating process for each economy and high degrees of heterogeneity. That said, the relationship with GDP having R-squared of 0.06, may indicated emerging economies may not need to rely on fossil fuels to as great an extent to achieve prosperity unlike developed ones.

In conclusion, my project produced effective visualization showing the challenges and heterogeneity of emerging economies transition to renewables. Whilst the aggregate statistical relations between variables of interest may have been weak, this was likely due to the differing characteristics of countries, as shown by the high degree of clustering in the data. This does present the opportunity to analyse individual countries in more depth, but to also include more countries in the sample and apply machine learning algorithms, such as k-means clustering, to categorise countries transitions. Further whilst single linear regression was chosen to aid visualization, combing multiple regression with feature engineering to select most impactful variables could provide greater insight, at the cost clear graphics.
